# Double-Entry Bookkeeping Basics

## What Is a Journal Entry?

A **journal entry** is the fundamental record of a financial transaction. Every event that affects a company's finances — a sale, a purchase, a payment — is captured as a journal entry in the **general journal** before being posted to individual accounts.

## The Accounting Equation

All of double-entry bookkeeping rests on one identity:

$$A = L + E$$

**Assets** (what you own) always equal **Liabilities** (what you owe) plus **Equity** (the owners' residual claim). Every journal entry must preserve this equation.

## Debits and Credits

Each journal entry has at least **two lines** — one debit and one credit. The total debits must always equal the total credits.

- **Debit (Dt)**: left side of an account
- **Credit (Ct)**: right side of an account

These are not "good" or "bad" — they are simply directions. Whether a debit increases or decreases an account depends on the account's **normal balance** (covered in a later section).

## Structure of a Journal Entry

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|------|---------|-------|--------|
| 2024-03-15 | `521` Bank | 1,000,000 | |
| | `701` Sales - Merchandise | | 1,000,000 |

Key rules:
1. **Every transaction** must have at least one debit and one credit
2. **Total debits = Total credits** — always
3. Entries are recorded in **chronological order**
4. Each entry includes a **date** and **description** (libellé)

## Why Double-Entry?

Single-entry bookkeeping (like a personal checkbook) only tracks cash. Double-entry captures the *full economic reality* — when you buy equipment, both your assets and your cash change. This built-in cross-check makes errors easier to detect and financial statements more reliable.
