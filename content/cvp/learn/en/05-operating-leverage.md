# Degree of Operating Leverage

## Definition

**Degree of operating leverage (DOL)** measures how sensitive operating income is to a change in sales volume.

$$\text{DOL} = \frac{\text{Total Contribution Margin}}{\text{Operating Income}}$$

If DOL = 4×, a **1% increase in sales volume** produces approximately a **4% increase in operating income** — and vice versa on the downside.

## The Fixed-Cost Connection

DOL is driven by cost structure. A company with lots of **fixed costs** has high DOL because once BEP is cleared, every additional sale flows straight to the bottom line (no new fixed-cost hurdle to clear). Conversely, a company where most costs are **variable** has low DOL — costs rise and fall nearly in lockstep with revenue, dampening profit swings.

## Industry Comparison

| Industry | Typical Cost Profile | Typical DOL |
|----------|---------------------|-------------|
| Software / SaaS | Mostly fixed (engineering, infrastructure) | High (5–10×+) |
| Retail grocery | Mostly variable (COGS) | Low (1–2×) |
| Airlines | Mostly fixed (fuel commitments, aircraft) | Very high |
| Professional services | Balanced | Moderate (2–4×) |

## Interpreting Near-BEP DOL

DOL becomes very large (approaching infinity) as operating income approaches zero. At the break-even point, a tiny change in sales creates a huge percentage change in profit — because the denominator is near zero. This is mathematically correct but operationally misleading: always read DOL alongside *absolute* profit, not in isolation.

## Why Operators Care

DOL is a planning tool. Before taking on more fixed costs (expanding capacity, signing long leases, hiring salaried staff), managers should understand that they're amplifying both upside and downside. A recession hurts a high-DOL business much more than a low-DOL one — the same 20% revenue drop could mean a 100% profit wipeout.
