# Indifference Point

## Definition

The **indifference point** is the sales volume at which two cost structures — for example "lease vs. buy" or "automate vs. outsource" — produce identical operating income.

$$Q^* = \frac{FC_A - FC_B}{VC_B - VC_A}$$

Above $Q^*$, the structure with the **higher fixed costs and lower variable cost per unit** wins (volume amplifies its advantage). Below $Q^*$, the structure with **lower fixed costs** wins (you don't need the volume to justify the overhead).

## A Typical Decision

A manufacturer considers two options:

- **Option A — Outsource**: $80,000 fixed cost, $45 variable cost per unit
- **Option B — In-house**: $120,000 fixed cost, $35 variable cost per unit

Indifference point: (120,000 − 80,000) / (45 − 35) = **4,000 units**

- If expected volume **< 4,000**, outsource (Option A).
- If expected volume **> 4,000**, in-house (Option B).
- At exactly 4,000, either works.

## Edge Cases

**Parallel lines** — If both options have the same variable cost per unit, the cost lines never cross. The option with lower fixed cost *dominates* at every volume. The playground flags this as "Cost lines are parallel."

**Dominated alternative** — When the math returns a negative or zero volume, it means one option is worse at every feasible volume (higher FC *and* higher VC). Never pick a dominated alternative; the playground shows "X dominates across all feasible volumes."

## What the Formula Assumes

Indifference analysis assumes:

1. **Fixed costs are genuinely fixed** within the planning range. If volumes push you into a new capacity bracket, fixed costs step up.
2. **Prices are identical across options.** If the outsourced version sells at a discount (because quality differs), you must compare full CVP outcomes, not just cost structures.
3. **No qualitative factors.** Control, quality, strategic positioning, and supplier risk often matter more than the crossover point.
