# Margin of Safety

## Definition

**Margin of safety (MoS)** measures how much sales can drop before the company reaches break-even — it's the cushion between current sales and the cliff.

$$\text{MoS (units)} = \text{Current Volume} - \text{BEP}$$

$$\text{MoS \%} = \frac{\text{Current Sales} - \text{Break-Even Sales}}{\text{Current Sales}} \times 100$$

## Why It Matters

A company with 40% margin of safety can lose nearly half its sales before operating at a loss. A company with 5% MoS has almost no buffer — a minor downturn pushes it into the red.

Investors, lenders, and managers use MoS to gauge **operational risk**. A business with a high MoS is resilient; one with a low MoS is fragile.

## Reading the Chart

On the classic CVP chart, the margin-of-safety overlay draws a bracket between the BEP point and the current operating volume. When current volume sits left of BEP (the loss zone), the bracket turns red and MoS becomes negative — the company isn't merely fragile, it's actively losing money.

## Rules of Thumb

- **MoS > 30%** — comfortable operating cushion
- **MoS 10–30%** — acceptable, but watch for demand shocks
- **MoS < 10%** — dangerously thin; consider price increases, cost cuts, or volume growth
- **MoS < 0** — currently operating at a loss

## Pairs With DOL

MoS and DOL (degree of operating leverage) are mathematical inverses:

$$\text{MoS \%} \times \text{DOL} \approx 100\% \text{ (when measured at OI)}$$

A high-MoS company has low DOL (profit changes slowly with volume). A low-MoS company has high DOL (profit whipsaws with every sales shift).
