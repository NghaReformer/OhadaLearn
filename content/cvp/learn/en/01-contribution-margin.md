# Contribution Margin

## What Is Contribution Margin?

The **contribution margin (CM)** is the amount each unit sold contributes toward covering fixed costs and generating profit.

$$\text{CM per unit} = \text{Price} - \text{Variable Cost}$$

$$\text{CM Ratio} = \frac{\text{CM per unit}}{\text{Price}}$$

## Why It Matters

Contribution margin is the foundation of all CVP analysis. Before a business can earn a profit, it must first cover its fixed costs — and every unit sold contributes CM toward that fixed-cost hurdle.

- A **positive CM** means each sale moves the company closer to profit.
- A **negative CM** means every sale deepens the loss — there's no price point where volume alone saves the business.
- The **CM ratio** lets you think in dollars-of-revenue rather than units. Each $1 of sales generates CM ratio × $1 of contribution.

## Example

A product sells for $50 with $30 in variable cost per unit.

- CM per unit = $50 − $30 = **$20**
- CM ratio = $20 / $50 = **40%**

If fixed costs are $100,000, then each unit sold chips $20 off the fixed-cost mountain. You need 5,000 units to climb the mountain entirely — the **break-even point**.

## Fixed vs Variable Costs — Why the Distinction

Only *variable* costs reduce the CM. Fixed costs sit outside the margin calculation because they don't change with volume — they're the hurdle you're trying to clear.

| Cost | Type | In CM? |
|------|------|--------|
| Raw materials | Variable | Yes |
| Sales commissions | Variable | Yes |
| Factory rent | Fixed | No |
| Salaried supervisors | Fixed | No |
| Utilities | Mixed — split first | Partially |

## Key Insight

CM isn't gross profit. Gross profit subtracts *all* COGS (fixed + variable). CM subtracts only the variable portion. That's why a company can show positive gross profit yet still lose money — its fixed costs may be higher than its total CM.
