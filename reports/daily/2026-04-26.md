### Issue 1 — Hardcoded `#fff` on themed backgrounds (medium)

| # | File | Line | Detail |
|---|------|------|--------|
| 1 | `src/lib/playgrounds/journal-entry/components/EntryHistory.svelte` | 400 | `.btn-confirm { color: #fff }` on `var(--error)` bg |
| 2 | `src/routes/[lang]/chart-of-accounts/+page.svelte` | 423 | `.framework-btn.active { color: #fff }` on `var(--accent)` bg |
| 3 | `src/routes/[lang]/chart-of-accounts/+page.svelte` | 458 | `.class-chip.active { color: #fff }` on `var(--accent)` bg |
| 4 | `src/routes/[lang]/learn/+page.svelte` | 202 | `color: #fff` on `var(--accent)` bg |

**Why it matters:** `--textPrimary` is `#eef0f6` (not pure white). When the theme evolves (e.g., a light-mode token set is added), every `#fff` instance will produce wrong contrast. The token system exists precisely to prevent this coupling.

**Suggested fix:** Add `textOnAccent: '#ffffff'` to `ThemeTokens.colors` in `src/lib/theme/tokens.ts`, expose it as `--text-on-accent`, and replace all four `color: #fff` occurrences with `color: var(--text-on-accent)`. Alternatively use `color: var(--text-primary)` if `#eef0f6` passes contrast on `--accent`/`--error` (verify with WCAG AA: 4.5:1 ratio needed).

### Issue 3 — Raw Tailwind-derived `rgba()` values in `ScheduleTable.svelte` style block (medium)

**File:** `src/lib/playgrounds/amortization/components/ScheduleTable.svelte`  
**Lines:** 215, 219, 241–242, 247–248, 260–261, 266–267

The style block uses raw numeric channel values that are copies of Tailwind palette entries:
- `rgba(245, 158, 11, …)` — amber-500 (token `--amber` exists)
- `rgba(34, 197, 94, …)` — green-500 (token `--green` exists)
- `rgba(239, 68, 68, …)` — red-500 (token `--error` exists)
- `rgba(59, 130, 246, …)` — blue-500 (no token exists)

CSS currently lacks a native way to alpha-compose a custom property's hex value, which is why these rgba() calls exist. The correct modern fix is to define the token colors as RGB channels (e.g. `--amber-rgb: 245 158 11`) and use `rgb(var(--amber-rgb) / 0.08)`. This avoids raw hex in component styles while staying theme-aware.

**Suggested fix:**  
1. Add `*Rgb` companion variables to `css-generator.ts` for the 4 semantic colors (`amber`, `green`, `error`, plus a new `info` token for the blue).  
2. Replace `rgba(245, 158, 11, 0.08)` with `rgb(var(--amber-rgb) / 0.08)` etc.  
3. Define `info: '#3b82f6'` (or equivalent) in `ThemeTokens` — currently blue has no token.

The link's accessible name is the translated string from `playgrounds.back`. This is WCAG-compliant as long as that translation resolves to descriptive text (e.g. "Back to playgrounds" rather than just "Back"). A bare "Back" is ambiguous when read by a screen reader out of context.

### Playground A — VAT / TVA Input-Output Reconciliation

**EN:** VAT Input-Output Reconciliation  
**FR:** Rapprochement TVA collectée / déductible

**Target learner:** Licence 2–3, practicing professional

**Pedagogical objective:** Many students correctly calculate TVA on individual invoices but fail to prepare the monthly/quarterly TVA declaration (CA3 equivalent in OHADA zone). This playground closes the gap between transactional TVA and declarative TVA — a common exam failure point.

**Core interaction:** Learner enters a set of sales invoices (HT amount + TVA rate) and purchase invoices (HT + TVA rate, deductibility %). The playground computes TVA collectée, TVA déductible, and net TVA payable/credit. Learner must categorise each purchase as fully deductible, partially deductible, or excluded.

**Minimum viable inputs/outputs:**
- Inputs: 3–6 sales lines (HT, rate), 3–6 purchase lines (HT, rate, deductibility %)
- Outputs: TVA collectée total, TVA déductible total, net balance, journal entries for settlement

**Why it complements existing playgrounds:** Journal-entry playground already exists; this playground generates the entries and lets students verify their own reconciliation first, then compare — a scaffolded path from concept to journal.

### Playground B — Inventory Valuation Methods Comparison

**EN:** Inventory Valuation — FIFO vs. Weighted Average Cost  
**FR:** Valorisation des stocks — FIFO vs. CMUP

**Target learner:** Licence 2, Licence 3

**Pedagogical objective:** SYSCOHADA prescribes weighted average cost (CMUP) as the primary method; IFRS also permits FIFO. Students routinely confuse the two and miscalculate closing stock values and COGS. The misconception is that FIFO and CMUP always produce the same gross profit — this playground disproves it with real numbers.

**Core interaction:** Learner enters a sequence of stock movements (opening balance, 3–5 purchase lots at different unit costs, 2–3 sales). The playground computes closing stock and COGS under both FIFO and CMUP in side-by-side columns. A difference badge highlights the profit impact.

**Minimum viable inputs/outputs:**
- Inputs: opening stock (qty, unit cost), purchase lines (date, qty, unit cost), sale lines (date, qty)
- Outputs: FIFO closing stock value, CMUP closing stock value, COGS comparison, profit differential

**Why it complements existing playgrounds:** Amortization and depreciation playgrounds cover asset tracking; this covers current asset tracking — the remaining major balance-sheet valuation category untouched.

### Playground C — Payroll & Social Charges Calculator (OHADA Zone)

**EN:** Payroll & Social Contributions — OHADA Zone  
**FR:** Bulletin de paie et charges sociales — zone OHADA

**Target learner:** Licence 3, Master, practicing professional

**Pedagogical objective:** OHADA-zone payroll is governed by country-specific CNPS (social security) rates, IRPP (income tax) progressive brackets, and profession/family-allowance deductions. Students from Cameroon, Côte d'Ivoire, and Senegal use different rate tables but the same SYSCOHADA journal structure. The playground makes both the calculation and the journal entry transparent.

**Core interaction:** Learner selects country (Cameroun / Côte d'Ivoire / Sénégal as MVP), enters gross salary, number of dependants, and employment category. The playground computes: gross → net breakdown (employee social charges, IRPP, family allowances), employer charges (CNPS employer share, industrial accident, family branch), and cost-to-company. Journal entry (641, 431, 447 accounts) auto-generated.

**Minimum viable inputs/outputs:**
- Inputs: country, gross monthly salary, dependants, category (cadre / non-cadre)
- Outputs: itemised deductions, net salary, employer total cost, SYSCOHADA journal entry

**Why it complements existing playgrounds:** No playground currently touches labour-cost accounting, which is a required module in all OHADA-zone university curricula and appears on professional certification exams.

### Playground D — Budget Variance Analysis

**EN:** Budget Variance Analysis  
**FR:** Analyse des écarts budgétaires

**Target learner:** Licence 3, Master 1

**Pedagogical objective:** Managerial accounting students often calculate variances mechanically (actual vs. budget) but cannot decompose them into volume and price components or interpret their sign (favourable / unfavourable). This is a critical gap before students enter management control roles.

**Core interaction:** Learner sets budget parameters (budgeted units, budgeted unit price, budgeted unit cost). Then enters actuals (actual units sold, actual unit revenue, actual unit cost). The playground decomposes the revenue variance into volume variance and price variance, and the cost variance into volume and efficiency components. Visual bars show F/U (favourable/unfavourable) in green/amber.

**Minimum viable inputs/outputs:**
- Inputs: budget (qty, unit price, unit variable cost, fixed costs), actuals (qty, unit price, unit variable cost, fixed costs actual)
- Outputs: total revenue variance (volume + price), total cost variance (volume + efficiency), operating profit variance, interpretation text

**Why it complements existing playgrounds:** CVP playground covers breakeven analysis; this playground is its natural sequel — what happens after the plan is set and reality differs. Together they cover the managerial accounting arc from planning to control.

### Playground E — Foreign Currency Transaction Accounting

**EN:** Foreign Currency Transactions — IAS 21 / SYSCOHADA  
**FR:** Opérations en devises — IAS 21 / SYSCOHADA

**Target learner:** Licence 3, Master 1, practicing professional

**Pedagogical objective:** Handling transactions denominated in USD, EUR, or CFA cross-currency (e.g. a Cameroonian company invoicing a Nigerian client in USD) requires translation at transaction date, re-measurement at closing rate, and recognition of exchange differences. Students consistently record the wrong account (487 — écart de conversion, vs. 776/676 — gains/losses on exchange) depending on whether the difference is unrealised or settled.

**Core interaction:** Learner enters a foreign-currency sale (FCY amount, transaction date rate), then a closing balance sheet date, then the settlement date rate. The playground walks through three journal entries: initial recognition, year-end re-measurement (deferred gain/loss via 487 account), and settlement (realised gain/loss). A toggle switches between SYSCOHADA treatment (prudence: only unrealised losses recognised at year-end) and IFRS (symmetric treatment).

**Minimum viable inputs/outputs:**
- Inputs: invoice amount (FCY), transaction rate, closing rate, settlement rate, currency pair
- Outputs: functional currency amounts at each date, exchange difference at year-end, realised gain/loss, three journal entries with account codes

**Why it complements existing playgrounds:** The existing format library already supports 16 currencies and locale-aware number parsing. This playground is the only one that would use the currency-pair formatting infrastructure meaningfully, and it covers a topic absent from all five current modules.
